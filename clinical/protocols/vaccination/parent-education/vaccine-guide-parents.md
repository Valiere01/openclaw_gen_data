## Welcome!

Vaccines are one of the safest and most effective ways to protect your child from serious diseases. This guide answers common questions and helps you understand the vaccination process.

---

## Why Vaccinate?

### The Facts

✅ **Vaccines work.** Diseases like polio, measles, and diphtheria that once sickened thousands of children are now rare because of vaccines.

✅ **Vaccines are safe.** All vaccines undergo years of testing before approval and continue to be monitored for safety.

✅ **Vaccines protect everyone.** When most people are vaccinated, it protects those who can't be vaccinated (babies too young, people with certain medical conditions).

✅ **Vaccines save lives.** According to the CDC, vaccines prevent 2-3 million deaths worldwide every year.

### What Happens Without Vaccines?

When vaccination rates drop, diseases come back:
- **Measles:** Outbreaks have occurred in communities with low vaccination rates
- **Whooping Cough (Pertussis):** Still circulates and can be deadly for infants
- **Hib:** Once the leading cause of bacterial meningitis in children

## The Vaccination Schedule

### Your Child's First Year

| Age | Vaccines | What They Protect Against |
|-----|----------|--------------------------|
| **Birth** | Hepatitis B | Liver disease |
| **2 months** | DTaP, IPV, Hib, PCV20, RV, HepB | Diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, polio, Hib, pneumococcal, rotavirus, hepatitis B |
| **4 months** | DTaP, IPV, Hib, PCV20, RV | Same as 2 months |
| **6 months** | DTaP, Hib, PCV20, RV, HepB, Flu* | Same as above + influenza |
| **9 months** | (Catch-up if needed) | - |
| **12 months** | MMR, VAR, HepA, PCV20, Hib | Measles, mumps, rubella, chickenpox, hepatitis A |

*Flu vaccine is given annually starting at 6 months

### Toddler and Preschool Years

| Age | Vaccines |
|-----|----------|
| **15-18 months** | DTaP, (Hib if needed), (PCV20 if needed) |
| **18-23 months** | HepA (2nd dose) |
| **4-6 years** | DTaP, IPV, MMR (2nd dose), VAR (2nd dose) |

### School Age and Teens

| Age | Vaccines |
|-----|----------|
| **11-12 years** | Tdap, MenACWY, HPV (2 doses) |
| **16-18 years** | MenACWY booster, MenB (optional), HPV (if not completed) |

## Common Questions

### Q: Can vaccines cause autism?

**A: No.** This concern came from a single study in 1998 that was later found to be fraudulent and was retracted. Multiple large studies involving millions of children have found **no link** between vaccines and autism.

### Q: My child is sick. Should we still vaccinate?

**A: Usually yes.** Mild illnesses (cold, low-grade fever, ear infection) are NOT reasons to delay vaccination. However, moderate or severe illness may require postponing. Your provider will advise.

### Q: Can too many vaccines overwhelm my child's immune system?

**A: No.** Children's immune systems handle thousands of germs every day. The antigens in vaccines are a tiny fraction of what children encounter naturally. Studies show that receiving multiple vaccines at once is safe.

### Q: Are natural infections better than vaccines?

**A: No.** Natural infections can cause serious complications:
- **Measles:** Can cause pneumonia, brain damage, death
- **Chickenpox:** Can cause skin infections, pneumonia, brain swelling
- **Whooping cough:** Can cause pneumonia, seizures, death in infants

Vaccines provide protection without these risks.

### Q: What about vaccine ingredients?

**A:** Vaccine ingredients are safe and necessary:
- **Aluminum:** Helps vaccine work better; babies get more aluminum from breast milk or formula
- **Formaldehyde:** Used to inactivate viruses; body naturally produces formaldehyde
- **Thimerosal:** Preservative (only in some flu vaccines); no evidence of harm; egg-free options available

### Q: My child had a reaction to a vaccine. Should we continue?

**A: Talk to your provider.** Most reactions are mild (fever, soreness). Serious reactions are extremely rare. Your provider can help determine if future doses are safe.

## Before Your Visit

### Prepare Your Child

**For Infants:**
- Feed before or after vaccination
- Bring a comfort item (blanket, pacifier)
- Hold and comfort during/after shots

**For Toddlers and Older Children:**
- Be honest: "You'll feel a quick pinch"
- Don't say "It won't hurt" (it might feel like a pinch)
- Bring distractions (book, toy, tablet)
- Practice deep breathing together
- Praise bravery afterward

## After Vaccination

### Common Side Effects

These are normal and mean the vaccine is working:

| Side Effect | What to Expect | How Long |
|-------------|----------------|----------|
| **Soreness at shot site** | Redness, swelling, tenderness | 1-3 days |
| **Low-grade fever** | Up to 101°F (38.3°C) | 1-2 days |
| **Fussiness** | Irritability, crying | 1-2 days |
| **Decreased appetite** | Less interested in eating | 1-2 days |
| **Tiredness** | Sleepier than usual | 1-2 days |

### Comfort Measures

✅ **Cool compress** on injection site  
✅ **Age-appropriate pain reliever** (acetaminophen or ibuprofen)*  
✅ **Extra cuddles and comfort**  
✅ **Cool fluids** and light clothing if feverish  
✅ **Rest** and quiet activities  

*Do NOT give aspirin to children. Consult your provider before giving pain relievers to infants under 6 months.

### When to Call the Doctor

Contact your provider if your child has:

🚨 **Fever over 103°F (39.4°C)**  
🚨 **Fever lasting more than 3 days**  
🚨 **Unusual crying** lasting more than 3 hours  
🚨 **Extreme sleepiness** or difficulty waking  
🚨 **Seizure** (shaking or staring spell)  
🚨 **Signs of allergic reaction:**
  - Hives or rash
  - Swelling of face, lips, or tongue
  - Difficulty breathing
  - Wheezing
  - Vomiting

**For severe reactions, call 911 immediately.**

## Vaccine Safety Monitoring

Vaccines are monitored more closely than any other medication:

### Before Approval
- **Years of laboratory research**
- **Clinical trials** with thousands of participants
- **FDA review** of all data

### After Approval
- **VAERS** (Vaccine Adverse Event Reporting System): Anyone can report side effects
- **VSD** (Vaccine Safety Datalink): Ongoing monitoring of millions of vaccine recipients
- **CISA** (Clinical Immunization Safety Assessment): Expert consultation on complex cases

### Who Monitors Safety?
- CDC (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention)
- FDA (Food and Drug Administration)
- AAP (American Academy of Pediatrics)
- Independent researchers worldwide

## Financial Assistance

### Vaccines for Children (VFC) Program

The VFC program provides **free vaccines** for children who:
- Are Medicaid-eligible
- Are uninsured
- Are American Indian or Alaska Native
- Are underinsured (insurance doesn't cover vaccines)

**Ask your provider if you qualify.**

### Insurance Coverage

- Most insurance plans cover all recommended vaccines at **no cost**
- The Affordable Care Act requires coverage of ACIP-recommended vaccines
- Check with your insurance provider for details
